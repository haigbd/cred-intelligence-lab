Validation
Why These Patterns Make Sense

Prediction Validation

While individual company predictions carry uncertainty (this is extrapolation, not observed data), the aggregate patterns produced by the model consistently match established industry knowledge:

  • B2C companies allocate 4% to influencer vs 4% for B2B — consistent with consumer brands investing heavily in Instagram/YouTube creator partnerships.
  • Highest marketing intensity: Non-Profit Organizations, Leisure, Real Estate — consumer-facing sectors that compete on brand spend the largest share of revenue on marketing, matching industry benchmarks.
  • Lowest marketing intensity: Education, Metals & Mining, Food & Beverages — capital-intensive and institutional sectors where sales are relationship-driven rather than marketing-driven.
  • Largest median budgets: Information Technology, Consumer Durable Goods, Leisure — these sectors contain the largest companies with the most aggressive marketing operations, consistent with publicly available advertising rankings.
  • Budget distribution is heavy-tailed — a small number of large companies account for a disproportionate share of total spend. This matches real-world patterns where the top 100 advertisers represent ~60% of US ad spend (Ad Age).
Performance
Why ML Over Simple Heuristics?

This is a predictive model that extrapolates marketing spend for companies that don't publicly disclose it. It is not ground truth — it's designed for relative comparisons, ranking, and benchmarking.

The key question: does the ML model capture any real signal, or would a simple heuristic (e.g. "predict the sector median") work just as well?

Heuristic baselines contain almost no predictive signal

We systematically tested every reasonable heuristic — predicting the global median, the sector median, sector+industry median, and sector+industry+customerType median. None exceeded R² = 0.10 in log-space. Most were near zero or negative. Even grouping by sector+industry — the most granular heuristic — explains at best 2–10% of variance.

The ML model, using 40 company features (financials, digital footprint, sector, funding stage), achieves R² of 0.27–0.73 — a 4–12x improvement over the best heuristic. This confirms the model is capturing real, multi-dimensional signal that simple groupings cannot.

The model uses 40 features per company — financials (revenue, EBITDA, gross margin), digital footprint (ad impressions, traffic, social), and company attributes (sector, size, public/private, funding) — to estimate marketing allocation.

This is a predictive model that extrapolates spend for the full universe of companies — most of which never publicly disclose marketing budgets. Estimates are most reliable for relative ranking within a sector and cohort-level patterns (e.g. "B2C companies spend more on influencer than B2B"). Individual company figures should be treated as order-of-magnitude predictions, not audited actuals.

Channel Definitions

Digital Advertising Display & social ad spend, estimated from Pathmatics data (scaled to approximate total digital). Does not include TV, print, radio, or OOH.
Influencer Creator partnerships on Instagram & YouTube, estimated from Upfluence data (scaled proxy).
Sponsorship Sponsorship deals (sports, events, entertainment), from proprietary sponsorship datasets.
Other / Residual Everything else: events, content marketing, PR, team costs, TV/print/radio/OOH, trade shows, etc. Computed as Total Marketing − tracked channels.
