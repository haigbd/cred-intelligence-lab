Model Methodology

The Marketing Breakdown Top-Down Allocation Model predicts how much a company spends on marketing and how that budget is allocated across advertising, influencer marketing, sponsorship, and residual channels. It is designed for companies where actual marketing spend data is unavailable — which is the vast majority of companies in the CRED database.

How it works:

  • Four LightGBM regression models are trained independently, each predicting a log-transformed target: total marketing expense, influencer spend, advertising spend, and sponsorship spend.
  • Each model uses 40 predictor features spanning financials (revenue, EBITDA, margins), scale metrics (employees, funding), digital footprint (web traffic, ad impressions, MAU), and categorical identifiers (sector, industry, public/private status).
  • Hyperparameter tuning is performed via Optuna (Bayesian optimization with TPE sampler and median pruning, 25 trials per target), and models are trained with 5-fold cross-validation with LightGBM early stopping.
  • All targets are log-transformed (log1p) because marketing spend spans several orders of magnitude — $100K for a small tech startup vs. $10B for a global CPG brand. Log space ensures the model treats proportional errors equally across company sizes.
  • The final top-down allocation step predicts total marketing expense first, then distributes it across channels using model-predicted weights blended with industry-benchmark priors (70% prior / 30% model) to correct for systematic measurement biases across data sources.

Why log-space metrics? A log-space MAE of 1.0 means predictions are typically off by a factor of e¹ ≈ 2.7x. In dollar terms, the model might predict $5M for a company that actually spends $2M or $14M. This sounds large, but marketing spend is one of the most opaque corporate figures — even analyst estimates routinely differ by 2–3x. The advertising model (R²=0.731, MAE=1.028) achieves strong performance, particularly given that no direct marketing budget data is used as input.

Data sources: Company financials (income statements), Pathmatics (digital ad spend), Upfluence (influencer pricing), EntityMetrics (sponsorship, funding, employees, digital), and CompanyFields (sector/industry classification).

Year-over-Year Consistency

A key question for any predictive model is: are the predictions stable across time? Below we compare each company's predicted marketing budget in 2025 vs 2026. Points clustering along the diagonal (y = x) indicate that the model assigns similar budgets year-over-year when company fundamentals haven't changed significantly.

Spearman ρ = 0.818 — The rank ordering of companies is highly stable across years. A company ranked in the top 100 in 2025 is overwhelmingly likely to remain in the top 100 in 2026. This is critical for prospecting and prioritization workflows.

Key Takeaways

  • Model stability is strong: Median YoY ratio of 0.9383x and average Spearman ρ of 0.842 across all metrics confirm that predictions are consistent and reliable for year-over-year comparisons.
  • Rankings are trustworthy: The rank ordering of companies by predicted marketing spend is highly preserved across years — important for prospecting and competitive benchmarking.
  • Advertising model is the strongest: With R²=0.731 and the most training data (83K rows from Pathmatics), the advertising spend sub-model drives the most reliable component of the allocation.
  • Large companies dominate the top: The top 30 predicted spenders are overwhelmingly well-known public companies with revenues in the tens of billions — validating that the model correctly captures the scale relationship between company size and marketing spend.
  • Company-level deltas are expected: Individual companies can show ±50–100% YoY changes, which is a natural consequence of (a) log-space modeling where small shifts amplify in dollar terms, and (b) legitimate changes in underlying financials between years.
